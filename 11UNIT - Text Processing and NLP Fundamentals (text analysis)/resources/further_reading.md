# Unit 11: Further Reading

## Text Processing and NLP Fundamentals

Annotated bibliography for deeper exploration of text processing and natural language processing topics.

---

## Foundational Texts

### 1. Jurafsky, D., & Martin, J. H. (2024). *Speech and Language Processing* (3rd ed.)

The definitive textbook for NLP, freely available online. Covers everything from regex through transformers. Essential reading for anyone pursuing NLP seriously. Chapters 2–3 on regular expressions and text normalisation are particularly relevant to this unit.

**URL**: https://web.stanford.edu/~jurafsky/slp3/

---

### 2. Bird, S., Klein, E., & Loper, E. (2009). *Natural Language Processing with Python*

The official NLTK book, providing practical introduction to NLP using Python. Though somewhat dated, it remains an excellent resource for understanding fundamental concepts. Available freely online.

**URL**: https://www.nltk.org/book/

---

### 3. Friedl, J. E. F. (2006). *Mastering Regular Expressions* (3rd ed.). O'Reilly Media.

The comprehensive guide to regular expressions across multiple languages. Deep coverage of regex engine internals, performance optimisation and advanced techniques. Essential for anyone writing complex patterns.

---

## Python-Specific Resources

### 4. Python re Module Documentation

The authoritative reference for Python's regex implementation. Includes complete syntax reference, examples and notes on Python-specific behaviours.

**URL**: https://docs.python.org/3/library/re.html

---

### 5. spaCy Documentation

Industrial-strength NLP library documentation. Covers tokenisation, POS tagging, named entity recognition and more. Emphasises production deployment and performance.

**URL**: https://spacy.io/usage

---

### 6. scikit-learn Text Feature Extraction

Documentation for sklearn's text processing utilities including CountVectorizer and TfidfVectorizer. Practical examples for converting text to numerical features.

**URL**: https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/feature_extraction.html#text-feature-extraction

---

## Academic Papers

### 7. Porter, M. F. (1980). An Algorithm for Suffix Stripping. *Program*, 14(3), 130–137.

The original paper describing the Porter stemming algorithm. Still widely cited and implemented. Understanding this paper provides insight into rule-based morphological processing.

---

### 8. Salton, G., & Buckley, C. (1988). Term-Weighting Approaches in Automatic Text Retrieval. *Information Processing & Management*, 24(5), 513–523.

Foundational paper on TF-IDF weighting schemes. Compares various term weighting approaches for information retrieval. Essential background for understanding why TF-IDF works.

---

### 9. Manning, C. D., Raghavan, P., & Schütze, H. (2008). *Introduction to Information Retrieval*. Cambridge University Press.

Comprehensive textbook on information retrieval including text processing, indexing and search. Chapters on text processing and indexing complement this unit's coverage. Freely available online.

**URL**: https://nlp.stanford.edu/IR-book/

---

## Unicode and Encoding

### 10. The Unicode Consortium. *The Unicode Standard*

The definitive reference for Unicode character encoding. Essential for understanding code points, normalisation forms and the complexities of multilingual text processing.

**URL**: https://unicode.org/standard/standard.html

---

### 11. Spolsky, J. (2003). The Absolute Minimum Every Software Developer Must Know About Unicode and Character Sets

Accessible introduction to character encoding issues. Explains why encoding matters and common pitfalls. Recommended starting point for developers new to Unicode.

**URL**: https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2003/10/08/the-absolute-minimum-every-software-developer-absolutely-positively-must-know-about-unicode-and-character-sets-no-excuses/

---

## Digital Humanities

### 12. Jockers, M. L. (2013). *Macroanalysis: Digital Methods and Literary History*. University of Illinois Press.

Demonstrates computational text analysis applied to literary history. Excellent example of how text processing enables new research questions in humanities disciplines.

---

### 13. Underwood, T. (2019). *Distant Horizons: Digital Evidence and Literary Change*. University of Chicago Press.

Explores how computational methods reveal patterns in literary history. Shows text processing techniques applied at scale to historical corpora.

---

## Interactive Learning

### 14. regex101.com

Interactive regex testing environment with explanation of pattern matching. Supports multiple regex flavours including Python. Invaluable for developing and debugging patterns.

**URL**: https://regex101.com/

---

### 15. RegexOne

Tutorial-based introduction to regular expressions with interactive exercises. Excellent for beginners learning regex fundamentals.

**URL**: https://regexone.com/

---

### 16. Explosion AI: spaCy Course

Free interactive course for learning spaCy. Covers tokenisation, linguistic features and pipeline components through hands-on exercises.

**URL**: https://course.spacy.io/

---

## Research Applications

### 17. Grimmer, J., & Stewart, B. M. (2013). Text as Data: The Promise and Pitfalls of Automatic Content Analysis Methods for Political Texts. *Political Analysis*, 21(3), 267–297.

Discusses methodological considerations for text analysis in social science research. Addresses validity, reliability and interpretation challenges.

---

### 18. Benoit, K., et al. (2018). quanteda: An R Package for the Quantitative Analysis of Textual Data. *Journal of Open Source Software*, 3(30), 774.

Though R-focused, this paper discusses corpus analysis methods applicable in any language. Useful for understanding research workflows.

---

## Recommended Reading Sequence

For those new to text processing:

1. Start with the NLTK Book (Bird et al.) for foundations
2. Explore regex101.com whilst reading Friedl for regex mastery
3. Read Manning et al. for information retrieval context
4. Study spaCy documentation for modern NLP practices
5. Explore digital humanities texts for research applications
